Word Families

Click on a language and search for a word to see if it is part of the 5,000 most frequent word families. The 'Family Members' column shows the full set of word forms that belong to each family. Choose 'Flemmas'1 to see inflected forms only. Choose 'Word Families'2 to see inflected forms and derived forms.

These sets can be adapted to create bespoke profiles using the 'Remove Inflected Forms' and 'Add Derived Forms' dropdowns on the MultilingProfiler page.

Notes:

  1. MultilingProfiler currently uses flemmas as the default lexical unit. Just like lemmas (i.e., level 2 of Bauer and Nation's (1993) word families; see Accessible Summary of the research), flemmas include a headword and its inflected forms (e.g., écrire, écris, écrit; arbeiten, arbeite, arbeitet; trabajar, trabajo, trabaja). Unlike lemmas, however, flemmas do not take part of speech into account. For instance, sourire (as a noun and a verb), mein (as an adjective and pronoun) and demasiado (as an adjective and adverb) are each one flemma. The two parts of speech they comprise are two lemmas.

    2. Derived forms of headwords and the inflected forms of these are flemmas in their own right. For instance, utile and inutile; arbeiten and Arbeit and trabajar and trabajador are separate flemmas. There are three exceptions to this:
    (i) French and Spanish place names (e.g., Paris and Espagne) are included in the same word families as their adjectival forms (e.g., parisienne and espagnol). German place names and their adjectival equivalents are separate word families.
    (ii) German adjectival participles (e.g., bildend, bildende, gebildet, gebildetem) are included in the same word families as the verb from which they are derived (e.g., bilden).
    (iii) Feminine forms of German agent nouns (e.g., Bürgerin) are included in the same word families as the masculine noun from which they are derived (e.g., Bürger).

  2. These word families include frequent, productive, predictable, and regular derived forms that align with the GCSE reading exams in England. You can view and (de)select these derivational patterns in the 'Derived Forms' dropdown list above the profiling window.
